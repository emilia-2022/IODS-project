Step 2: Hypotheses

Variable 1: Going Out

The first hypothesis to be examined is based on the variable going out with friends. Essentially, my hypothesis is that those who go out with their friends more often will be more prone to high alcohol use. I assume that drinking alcohol is a social bonding activity and this is why those who go out more frequently are more likely to have higher use.

Variable 2: Study Time

The second hypothesis is related to the variable study time. My hypothesis is that those who are studying more each week will have a lower chance of having high alcohol use. This hypothesis is purely based on my assumption that if you are spending the majority of your time studying, you will not have time to develop a high-use alcohol habit.

Variable 3: Health

The third hypothesis relates to the health variable. As it could potentially exacerbate existing poor health I hypothesize that those with poor health are less likely to have high alcohol use.

Variable 4: Sex

The final hypothesis focuses on the relationship between high alcohol use and sex. My hypothesis is that male students will have higher rates of alcohol use.

Interpretations

From the numerical summaries we can see that those with high-use alcohol consumption are more likely to be male, go out more and spend less time studying. We can also see from the first summary table that there are 259 students who are not high-use and 111 who are.

It appears that those who go out more often are more likely to have higher alcohol consumption. This is confirmed in previous summary table the higher means present in the going out (3.73 for True, 2.853 for False). These results are significant at a p-value of p = <0.001.

As for study time, the majority of those with high alcohol use study below 5 hours a week (42 for under 2 hours and 57 for under 5 hours). We can also see this in mean summary with a 1.77 average for those with high-use and a 2.16 average for those who do not have high-use. These results are significant at p= <0.001.

With a p value of p = 0.134, the only non-statistically significant results are those related to health. It can be observed that there is not a large difference between those who did not drink 3.5 and those who did 3.7. Perhaps then, in the context of this data set, one’s health does not have a large effect on whether or not one drinks a lot.

Sex, as it is a character, was not covered in the first summary. However, in the second summary it can be observed that there males represent 63.1% of the high-use drinkers whereas females represent 36.9%. Rather intuitively these results are flipped with those who are not high-use. These results are significant at p= <0.001 which makes it significant at a 99% CI.

Interpretations

From this model we can see that three of the four explanatory variables are statistically significant. These three variables are significant at different confidence intervals however, and only going out is significant at the 99% threshold. The other two variables, study time and sex, are significant with a 95% confidence interval (p =3.03e-10 and a 90% CI (p =0.01073) respectively.

From the coefficients we can see that the effect of going out on high alcohol use is an increase in the risk for high-alcohol use with the more on goes out (0,75535). For study time, as it is negative coefficient, for each increase in time spent studying there will be a reduction in the risk for high alcohol use (-0.49173). As for health, there is no significant effect. Finally, for sex, if the sex is male it will result in a risk increase (0.67717).

Final Interpretations and Model/Hypotheses Comparison

A generous reading of the confusion matrix and the performance of the model would say that the model predicts false values correctly 9/10 of the time and positive values correctly 1/2 of the time. I do not believe that my simple guessing strategies would be able to reach this level of accuracy. When guessing, I often rely on broad generalizations (those who go out must drink more) to reach my conclusions. My hypotheses were thus oversimplified and did not reveal the nuance in the data. A clear example was that those who go out often have a 50% chance of having high alcohol use or not. Therefore, in order to reach the model accuracy, I would need to take more factors into account during my guessing. Yes, it is true that male students do drink more but is how much they drink proportionally to their weight taken into account? Therefore, while a simple guessing strategy can perhaps point the researcher which way to look, I do not think it can predict results as accurately as a well-built model can.
